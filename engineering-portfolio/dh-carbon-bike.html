Carbon Fiber Downhill MTB — Seattube Lug Design

Built with Bike Builders of Berkeley. Focus: a structurally-critical Seattube lug designed in CAD, validated with FEA in Fusion 360, and produced via metal 3D printing.

Skills used:
Fusion 360 CAD + FEA Carbon layup + bonding interfaces Design for metal AM Load paths + safety factors Iterate → build → test

Overview

This project was about building a downhill-capable carbon frame with a focus on the Seattube lug—the part that sees major combined loads from braking, impacts, and steering inputs. We designed the lug geometry to create clean load paths into the frame tubes, maintain bearing alignment, and provide robust bonding surfaces for carbon interfaces.

I worked on the seattube lug from concept to manufacturing: Doing CAD design, hand calculations, FEA setup, result interpretation, and iterative geometry updates. Once we completed the design, the lug model was sent for metal 3D printing later getting bonded into the frame build.

Engineering focus: load cases (braking + impacts), stiffness vs. weight tradeoffs, printability, and maximum bond-area for carbon assembly.

Seattube Lug FEA (Fusion 360)

We modeled the lug with realistic constraints at the tube interfaces and applied representative load cases for downhill riding (hard braking moments + frontal impacts). The goal was to reduce peak stresses at transitions, control deflection at the seattube bores, and keep geometry friendly for printing and post-processing.

  • Load cases: braking torque about the seattube, impact-like force at fork/axle direction, steering-induced moments
  • What we watched: stress concentrations near tube junctions, seattube bore deflection/misalignment, thin-wall risk areas
  • Design changes: fillet + gusset tuning, wall thickness where it matters, smoother transitions to spread stress
  • Manufacturing constraints: avoid un-supportable overhangs, maintain minimum feature thickness, preserve post-machining access
Notes: This project was treated as a real world engineering task, including timely deadlines, CAD freezes, and a push to the finish line for Sea Otter 2025
