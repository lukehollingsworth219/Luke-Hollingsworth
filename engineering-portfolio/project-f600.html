F600 Racecar — Coming Soon

A self-built, low-cost but reliable Formula 600-style racecar to push my engineering skills into the real world — moving from an F600 sim racing series to actual wheel-to-wheel racing.

Role: design + build Tech: Fusion 360 ESP32 LTspice Python
Focus areas:
Chassis stiffness Powertrain packaging Embedded telemetry Test → iterate → validate Track driving development

Short summary

This project is about building a “cheap but not sketchy” racecar: simple, serviceable, and designed around reliability. I’m using it as a structured engineering exercise—requirements first, then design, analysis, fabrication, testing, and iteration.

Goals

  • Build a real racecar that’s reliable enough to drive hard, often.
  • Translate sim learnings into real-world vehicle dynamics + racecraft.
  • Design and validate subsystems (chassis, power, electronics) with real data.
  • Make it easy to service between sessions: modular, accessible, documented.

Constraints

  • Low-cost parts and fabrication methods.
  • Safety and reliability over peak performance.
  • Lightweight packaging with clean load paths.
  • Design choices driven by testability and iteration speed.

What makes it interesting: it’s a full-stack engineering build—CAD, structures, powertrain layout, embedded electronics, and data-driven testing—plus the feedback loop of actual track driving.

Highlights

Chassis: tube-frame with triangulation for torsional stiffness, designed around accessible weld joints and serviceability.

Power: motorcycle engine package, chain drive, and differential setup with focus on reliability and cooling.

Electronics: ESP32 dash, strain gauges, and data logging to capture what the car is doing (not just what it feels like).

“Coming soon” roadmap: chassis fabrication → rolling chassis → powertrain integration → electronics + dash → first shakedown testing → iteration.
